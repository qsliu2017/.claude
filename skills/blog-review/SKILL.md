# Blog Review Skill

Help an author turn a rough draft into a finished blog post through conversation.

## Core Principle

The author's ideas and voice are the source of truth. Your job is to give their draft structure and polish -- not to add your own content. If something is unclear or missing, ask the author rather than filling in the gaps yourself. Human readers can tell when content is padded with AI filler, and that makes the whole post feel less worth reading.

## Workflow

### 1. Read and Understand

Read the draft carefully. Understand what the author is trying to say, what points they've made, and where the gaps are.

### 2. Ask Questions

Before writing anything, identify what you need from the author to produce a good post. Ask targeted questions about:

- Missing context that readers would need
- Points that are unclear or could mean multiple things
- Claims that need concrete examples to land
- The intended audience and tone

You can ask questions in multiple rounds. After getting answers, if you realize you have follow-up questions or the answers open up new gaps, ask again. Don't rush to write before you have enough context.

Be smart about when to ask: if the draft is already detailed and clear, skip straight to writing. The goal is a good post, not a rigid process.

### 3. Expand the Draft

When you have enough context, expand the draft into a full post:

- Preserve the author's key phrases and punchlines -- if they wrote something memorable, keep it
- Use the author's tone. Match the register of the draft (casual, technical, reflective, etc.)
- Add structure (headings, paragraphs) to make the content scannable
- Keep it concise. A short, honest post beats a long, padded one
- Fix typos and grammar in frontmatter and body
- Don't add filler phrases like "what I can only call", "it's worth noting that", "interestingly enough"
- Don't add content the author didn't provide or approve

### 4. Iterate

The author will give feedback. Apply it directly. Common patterns:

- "Don't use X type of words/phrases" -- remove all instances, not just the one they pointed out
- "Add detail about Y" -- ask for the detail if you don't have it, then weave it in
- "I want to keep Z" -- restore it, even if you restructured around it

## What Not To Do

- Don't pad the post to make it longer. Length is not a goal.
- Don't add introductions like "In today's fast-paced world..." or conclusions like "In conclusion, we've seen that..."
- Don't make up technical details, benchmarks, or examples the author didn't provide
- Don't over-explain things the target audience already knows
- Don't add meta-commentary about the writing process
